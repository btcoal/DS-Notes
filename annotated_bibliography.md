# Autonomous Driving Safety and Injury Risk Modeling

## ADS Crash Rate Benchmarking and Safety Evaluation

**Authors:** K. D. Kusano, J. M. Scanlon, Y.-H. Chen, T. L. McMurry, R. Chen, T. Gode, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Comparison of Waymo rider-only crash data to human benchmarks at 7.1 million miles," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 25, no. sup1, pp. S66–S77, 2024, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2024.2380786.

This article presents a retrospective safety impact assessment of the Waymo Driver, an SAE Level 4 automated driving system operating in rider-only mode across Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles. Using crash data from NHTSA's Standing General Order (SGO) covering 7.14 million rider-only miles through October 2023, the authors construct benchmark comparisons against human crash rates at three severity levels: any property damage or injury, police-reported, and any injury reported. The results demonstrate an 80% reduction in any-injury-reported crashed vehicle rates (0.6 vs. 2.80 incidents per million miles) and a 55% reduction in police-reported rates (2.1 vs. 4.68 IPMM). The study carefully addresses methodological challenges including differences in crash reporting thresholds between ADS (SGO) and human (police-reported) data sources, underreporting bias, and selection bias arising from differing operational design domains. Statistically significant reductions were found for police-reported and any-injury-reported outcomes, while the any-property-damage-or-injury comparisons yielded mixed results due to difficulties in establishing matching human benchmarks at that severity level. The article positions these retrospective findings as one component of a broader safety case approach for ADS deployment.

**Authors:** K. D. Kusano, J. M. Scanlon, Y.-H. Chen, T. L. McMurry, R. Chen, T. Gode, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Comparison of Waymo Rider-Only crash rates by crash type to human benchmarks at 56.7 million miles," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, 2025, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2025.2493582.

This article extends the earlier work at 7.1 million miles, examining Waymo's rider-only crash rates over a substantially larger exposure of 56.7 million miles, disaggregated by crash type. The authors compare ADS crash rates to human benchmarks for specific crash configurations—including rear-end, angle, sideswipe, and other collision types—across Phoenix, San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Austin. This crash-type stratification enables a more granular understanding of where the ADS demonstrates the greatest safety advantages relative to human driving, and where challenges remain. The increased mileage provides substantially greater statistical power, yielding tighter confidence intervals and enabling detection of smaller effect sizes. The methodology maintains consistency with the authors' earlier benchmark framework while incorporating dynamic benchmark adjustments to account for the evolving operational design domain. The results contribute to the growing body of evidence on ADS safety performance and represent the largest published retrospective analysis of Waymo's rider-only crash data to date.

**Authors:** J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, Y.-H. Chen, T. L. McMurry, R. Chen, T. Gode, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Benchmarks for retrospective automated driving system crash rate analysis: A national and regional comparison using adjusted crash rates on freeways and non-freeway surface streets," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, 2025, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2025.2489571.

This article develops human crash rate benchmarks specifically designed for evaluating ADS performance, with separate estimates for freeways and non-freeway surface streets. The authors construct these benchmarks using police-reported crash data at both national and regional levels, adjusting for known biases including underreporting rates that differ by crash severity and road type. The separation of freeway and surface-street benchmarks is a critical methodological advance, as ADS operational design domains often encompass specific road types, and combining all road types into a single benchmark can introduce substantial confounding. The article presents crash rates per vehicle mile traveled (VMT) for different severity levels, enabling researchers and regulators to select appropriate comparison points based on the specific ADS deployment context. The work underscores how benchmark selection and adjustment methodology can dramatically influence conclusions about ADS safety performance.

**Authors:** J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, R. Chen, T. L. McMurry, T. Gode, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Benchmarks for retrospective automated driving system crash rate analysis: An update with expanded data and new crash rate estimation methodology," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 25, no. sup1, pp. S78–S90, 2024, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2024.2380782.

This article provides an updated and expanded benchmark methodology for retrospective ADS crash rate evaluation, building upon earlier benchmark estimation approaches. The authors refine their crash rate estimation methodology to better account for systematic biases in both ADS and human crash data, including differences in crash reporting thresholds, geographic variability, and temporal trends. The expanded dataset incorporates more recent crash and exposure data, improving the precision and applicability of the benchmarks. The article also introduces a more rigorous statistical framework for quantifying uncertainty in benchmark estimates, which is essential for drawing valid conclusions about whether observed differences between ADS and human crash rates are statistically significant. The work serves as a companion to the 7.1-million-mile crash rate comparison study and provides the foundational benchmarks against which ADS safety performance is evaluated.

**Authors:** Y.-H. Chen, J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, T. L. McMurry, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Dynamic benchmarks for automated driving system crash rate analysis," 

**Journal:** arXiv:2410.08903v1, 2024.

This article introduces the concept of dynamic benchmarks for ADS crash rate analysis—benchmarks that automatically adjust as the ADS operational design domain (ODD) evolves over time. Static benchmarks become less appropriate as an ADS expands to new geographic areas, road types, or operating conditions, because the relevant human crash rate comparison shifts accordingly. The dynamic benchmark framework proposed here uses a weighted combination of location-specific and road-type-specific human crash rates, with weights determined by the actual distribution of ADS miles across these dimensions. The methodology accounts for the fact that an ADS operating primarily on urban surface streets in San Francisco faces a fundamentally different crash risk environment than one operating on suburban arterials in Phoenix. As the fleet's geographic and ODD composition changes, the benchmark automatically recalibrates. This is particularly important for growing ADS deployments that are continually expanding their service territories and adds methodological rigor to retrospective safety evaluations.

## Safety Cases and Assessment Frameworks

**Authors:** F. M. Favarò, J. M. Scanlon, T. Victor, and K. D. Kusano, 

**Title:** "A safety case approach for the safety determination of automated driving," 

**Journal:** *Journal of Safety Research*, vol. 92, pp. 344–357, 2026, doi: 10.1016/j.jsr.2025.01.001.

This article proposes a structured safety case framework for determining whether an ADS can be considered safe enough for deployment. Drawing on safety case methodologies used in aviation, nuclear, and other safety-critical industries, the authors adapt these approaches to the unique challenges of autonomous driving. The framework integrates multiple forms of evidence—including simulation results, closed-course testing, and real-world operational data—into a coherent argumentative structure with explicit claims, subclaims, and supporting evidence. The article addresses the fundamental challenge of ADS safety determination: that no single test or metric is sufficient to demonstrate safety, and instead a holistic body of evidence must be assembled and evaluated. The authors discuss how different types of evidence complement each other at different stages of the ADS development lifecycle, from early design through full-scale deployment, and how the safety case must evolve as operational experience accumulates. The work provides a conceptual foundation that unifies the various retrospective and prospective safety evaluation methods described in other articles in this collection.

**Authors:** S. Schnelle, F. M. Favarò, J. M. Scanlon, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Guidance for assessment of safety case claims and evidence for automated driving," 

**Journal:** arXiv:2506.09929v1, 2025.

This article provides practical guidance for evaluating the credibility and sufficiency of claims and evidence within an ADS safety case. Building on the safety case framework described by Favarò et al. (2026), the authors address a critical gap: how to assess whether the evidence presented actually supports the safety claims being made, and to what degree. The guidance covers assessment criteria for different types of evidence, including how to evaluate the methodological quality of retrospective crash analyses, the representativeness of simulation test suites, and the completeness of hazard analyses. The article also discusses common pitfalls in safety case construction, such as confirmation bias in evidence selection, over-reliance on a single evidence type, and failure to adequately address known limitations. The work is intended for use by both ADS developers constructing safety cases and by regulators, insurers, and other stakeholders tasked with evaluating them, and represents an important step toward standardizing the safety determination process for autonomous vehicles.

**Authors:** J. M. Scanlon, E. R. Teoh, D. G. Kidd, K. D. Kusano, J. Bärgman, G. Chi-Johnston, L. Di Lillo, F. M. Favarò, C. A. Flannagan, H. Liers, B. Lin, M. Lindman, S. McLaughlin, M. A. Perez, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "RAVE checklist: Recommendations for overcoming challenges in retrospective safety studies of automated driving systems," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 26, no. 5, pp. 608–621, 2025, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2024.2435620.

This article presents the RAVE (Retrospective Automated Vehicle Evaluation) checklist, a set of fifteen recommendations developed by a multi-stakeholder working group of traffic safety experts from academia, insurance, and industry. The recommendations are organized around three pillars: quality and validity, transparency, and interpretation. Quality and validity recommendations address data compatibility, conservative analytical defaults, directly measurable outcomes, outcome data transformations, and uncertainty quantification. Transparency recommendations cover citation of data sources, provision of descriptive statistics, description of the ADS deployment, documentation of analysis decisions, and documentation of limitations. Interpretation recommendations address building upon prior research, developing specific research questions, ensuring conclusions reflect study design, exercising caution across severity levels, and presenting rates in incidents per exposure units. The checklist was motivated by the recognition that as ADS deployments grow, there will be an increasing body of retrospective safety evaluations performed by researchers with varying levels of domain expertise, and establishing best practices early is essential for maintaining scientific rigor and public trust. The RAVE checklist provides a much-needed standardization framework for this emerging field.

**Authors:** K. D. Kusano, J. M. Scanlon, Y.-H. Chen, T. L. McMurry, E. R. Teoh, T. Gode, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "A conflict typology framework for automated driving system safety evaluation," 

**Journal:** in *Proc. 27th Int. Tech. Conf. Enhanced Safety Vehicles (ESV)*, Paper No. 27ESV-000328, Yokohama, Japan, 2023.

This article introduces a conflict typology framework designed for the systematic evaluation of ADS safety performance beyond simple crash rate comparisons. The framework categorizes traffic conflicts—near-crash events where a collision was narrowly avoided—into distinct types based on the geometries, actor types, and pre-crash dynamics involved. By analyzing conflicts in addition to crashes, the framework enables a richer understanding of ADS driving behavior, particularly because crashes are statistically rare events and conflicts provide a much larger sample for analysis. The typology is designed to be applicable across different ADS platforms and operational design domains, facilitating standardized comparisons. The authors demonstrate how the framework can be applied to real-world ADS operational data to identify patterns in how the ADS interacts with other road users, which conflict configurations arise most frequently, and how the ADS responds to potential collision threats. This conflict-based approach complements crash rate analysis by offering insights into the ADS's proactive safety performance.


## Injury Risk Modeling

**Authors:** A. Schubert, E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, T. L. McMurry, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Development of pedestrian injury risk curves from GIDAS data for automated driving system evaluation," 

**Journal:** *Accident Analysis & Prevention*, vol. 190, p. 107177, 2023, doi: 10.1016/j.aap.2023.107177.

This article develops pedestrian injury risk curves using data from the German In-Depth Accident Study (GIDAS) to support the safety evaluation of automated driving systems. The authors employ logistic regression models to estimate the probability of pedestrian injury at various severity levels (MAIS 2+F through 5+F) as a function of vehicle impact speed, pedestrian age, and other biomechanically relevant variables. The use of GIDAS data provides a large, well-documented, and representative sample of pedestrian-vehicle collisions with detailed reconstruction information. The resulting injury risk curves enable prospective safety assessments of ADS by allowing researchers to estimate the expected injury outcome of simulated or counterfactual collisions given the predicted impact speed profile. This is particularly valuable because ADS safety evaluation often involves simulating the ADS response to reconstructed real-world crash scenarios, and injury risk curves provide the link between the physical collision parameters and the expected human harm. The article carefully addresses statistical considerations including model selection, goodness-of-fit, and the influence of GIDAS sampling weights on the estimated risk functions.

**Authors:** A. Schubert, E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, T. L. McMurry, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Bridging the gap between accident research and ADS safety evaluation: Development of cyclist injury risk curves," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, 2024, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2024.2367016.

This article extends the pedestrian injury risk modeling framework to cyclists, developing injury risk curves for bicycle-vehicle collisions using GIDAS data. The authors adapt the methodology previously applied to pedestrians, accounting for the biomechanical differences between pedestrian and cyclist impacts—including the role of the bicycle as an intermediary structure, differences in impact geometry, and the influence of cyclist speed on collision dynamics. The resulting injury risk functions estimate the probability of cyclist injury at the MAIS 2+F through 5+F severity levels as a function of vehicle impact speed, cyclist age, and an effective collision speed parameter that accounts for the angular relationship between the vehicle and cyclist trajectories. The concept of effective collision speed is a notable methodological contribution, as it captures the directional dependence of injury severity in a way that simple relative speed does not. These cyclist injury risk curves are complementary to the pedestrian curves and together provide a comprehensive set of vulnerable road user (VRU) injury risk models for use in ADS safety evaluations, including prospective simulation-based assessments where the ADS's ability to mitigate VRU collision severity is a key concern.

**Authors:** A. Schubert, E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, T. L. McMurry, and T. Unger, 

**Title:** "A mechanistic approach to modeling omnidirectional motorcyclist injury risk," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 26, no. sup1, pp. S121–S131, 2025, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2025.2570829.

This article develops injury risk models for motorcyclist collisions with passenger vehicles across all planar crash configurations, using data from the German In-Depth Accident Study (GIDAS). The study advances the state-of-the-art by incorporating biomechanically relevant predictor variables—including age via a spline function, sex, and a geometric-based assessment of potential normal projection—alongside a novel speed parameter termed Effective Collision Speed. This speed metric combines passenger vehicle and motorcycle speeds while accounting for reduced engagement associated with frictional effects in side impacts, providing a more physically meaningful predictor of injury severity than simple relative speed. The dataset comprises 2,499 passenger vehicle-to-motorcycle collisions. Key findings include that age significantly influenced injury risk at the MAIS 2+F and MAIS 3+F levels, potential normal projection was associated with higher injury risk (significant only for MAIS 2+F), and Effective Collision Speed emerged as the sole significant predictor for higher severity levels. The article also analyzes the impact of reweighting the GIDAS data to German national statistics, addressing the database's known bias toward severe and fatal collisions. These models extend the suite of VRU injury risk functions to motorcyclists, complementing the existing pedestrian and cyclist models.

**Authors:** E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, S. Kang, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Baseline vulnerable road user injury risk models for automated driving system evaluation," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 25, no. sup1, pp. S108–S118, 2024, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2024.2380789.

This article presents a unified set of baseline injury risk models for vulnerable road users (VRUs)—including pedestrians, cyclists, and other non-motorized road users—intended for use in evaluating ADS safety performance. The models estimate the probability of injury at different severity thresholds as a function of vehicle impact speed and VRU characteristics, providing a baseline against which the potential injury mitigation benefits of an ADS can be measured. By establishing what injury outcomes would be expected in the absence of ADS intervention, these models enable counterfactual analyses that quantify how much harm the ADS prevents (or fails to prevent) in collision scenarios. The article integrates data from multiple sources and harmonizes the modeling approach across different VRU types to ensure consistency. The baseline models are a critical component of the prospective safety evaluation methodology, as they allow researchers to translate simulated collision parameters into expected injury outcomes, thereby connecting the ADS's driving behavior to its ultimate safety impact on vulnerable road users.

**Authors:** E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Vulnerable road user pre-crash kinematics and injury risk during evasive manoeuvring," 

**Journal:** in *Proc. 2024 Int. Research Council on Biomechanics of Injury (IRCOBI)*, Paper No. 24117, Florence, Italy, 2024.

This article investigates the pre-crash kinematics and resulting injury risk experienced by vulnerable road users who attempt evasive maneuvers prior to vehicle impact. Using naturalistic driving study data and detailed kinematic reconstruction, the authors examine how VRU evasive actions—such as jumping, turning, or accelerating—alter the collision geometry and consequent injury severity compared to scenarios where the VRU does not react. The analysis reveals that evasive maneuvers can substantially change the impact configuration, including the wrap-around distance, the relative velocity vector, and the body region first contacted. The injury risk implications of these kinematic changes are quantified using the previously developed injury risk curves. This work is relevant to ADS evaluation because simulation-based safety assessments must decide whether to model VRU evasive behavior or assume a static VRU, and this choice can significantly affect the predicted injury outcomes. The article provides empirical evidence to inform this modeling decision and highlights the importance of realistic VRU behavioral models in prospective ADS safety evaluations.

**Authors:** E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Cyclist dooring events: Kinematic analysis and crash characteristics,"

**Journal:** *Proc. 2023 Int. Research Council on Biomechanics of Injury (IRCOBI)*, Paper No. 23112, Cambridge, UK, 2023.

This article presents a kinematic analysis and characterization of cyclist dooring events—collisions that occur when a vehicle occupant opens a door into the path of an approaching cyclist. Using naturalistic and crash reconstruction data, the authors document the typical kinematics of dooring events, including approach speeds, reaction times, impact configurations, and post-impact trajectories. Dooring is a particularly relevant crash type for ADS evaluation because ride-hailing vehicles frequently stop to pick up or drop off passengers, creating regular door-opening scenarios in the presence of cyclists. The article quantifies the injury risk associated with different dooring configurations and identifies the key parameters that determine severity, such as cyclist speed, door opening angle, and whether the cyclist strikes the door edge or the door panel. The findings inform both ADS operational procedures (e.g., passenger notification systems, selection of safe drop-off locations) and simulation-based safety assessments that must account for this crash mode. This is one of the few studies to systematically analyze dooring events with detailed kinematic data.

**Authors:** E. T. Campolettano, J. M. Scanlon, K. D. Kusano, T. L. McMurry, and T. Victor,

**Title:** "Pedestrian injury risk estimation from naturalistic driving study data using dash camera video analysis," 

**Journal:** *Proc. 27th Int. Tech. Conf. Enhanced Safety Vehicles (ESV)*, Paper No. 27ESV-000075, Yokohama, Japan, 2023.

This article develops a method for estimating pedestrian injury risk using naturalistic driving study (NDS) data captured through dash camera video. The approach addresses a key challenge in VRU injury risk modeling: obtaining representative pre-crash kinematic data that reflects real-world conditions rather than relying solely on police-reported crash reconstructions. By analyzing dash camera footage from NDS databases, the authors extract pedestrian position, speed, and trajectory information in the moments before vehicle-pedestrian interactions, and apply injury risk curves to estimate the potential injury severity. The methodology enables large-scale analysis of pedestrian exposure patterns and near-miss events, providing a richer picture of pedestrian risk than crash data alone. The article discusses the technical challenges of extracting accurate kinematic measurements from monocular video, including perspective correction, distance estimation, and speed calibration. The NDS-based approach is complementary to GIDAS-based injury risk curves and provides an alternative pathway for generating the input data needed for ADS safety evaluations.


**Authors:** K. Kusano and T. Victor, 

**Title:** "Methodology for determining maximum injury potential for automated driving system evaluation," 

**Journal:** *Traffic Injury Prevention*, vol. 23, no. sup1, pp. S224–S227, 2022, doi: 10.1080/15389588.2022.2125231.

This article proposes a methodology for estimating the maximum injury potential of a traffic conflict scenario, defined as the worst-case probability of a MAIS 3+ injury assuming the actors take no evasive action. The method uses three components: counterfactual trajectory simulation, a momentum-impulse collision model, and a continuous omnidirectional injury risk function. At each timestep, counterfactual trajectories are generated by applying small perturbations to each actor's acceleration and steering inputs. If these counterfactual trajectories result in a collision, a momentum-impulse model estimates the resulting delta-V and principal direction of force (PDOF), which are then fed into the injury risk function to estimate the probability of serious injury. The maximum injury potential across all counterfactual simulations defines the scenario's severity. This methodology addresses a fundamental question in scenario-based ADS testing: whether a given test scenario is actually capable of producing a serious injury if the ADS fails to respond appropriately. Scenarios with low maximum injury potential are less informative for evaluating ADS safety, regardless of the ADS's performance. The article demonstrates the approach using a left-turn-across-path scenario parameterized with real-world crash data distributions.



## Adaptive Sampling for Safety Evaluation

**Authors:** A. Sinha, M. O'Kelly, H. Zheng, R. Mangharam, J. Duchi, and R. Tedrake, 

**Title:** "Rate-informed discovery of partially observed systems using Bayesian adaptive multifidelity sampling,"

**Journal:** *Proc. Conf. Robot Learning (CoRL)*, 2024.

This article presents BAMS (Bayesian Adaptive Multifidelity Sampling), a methodology for efficiently discovering rare failure events in autonomous vehicle systems by leveraging multiple levels of simulation fidelity. ADS safety evaluation faces a fundamental challenge: catastrophic failures are extremely rare, making brute-force Monte Carlo sampling prohibitively expensive. BAMS addresses this by maintaining a Bayesian surrogate model over the parameter space of driving scenarios and using an acquisition function that balances exploration of uncertain regions with exploitation of regions likely to contain failures. The multifidelity aspect allows the algorithm to use cheap, low-fidelity simulations to broadly explore the scenario space and expensive, high-fidelity simulations to confirm and precisely characterize identified failure modes. The "rate-informed" component integrates prior knowledge about failure rates to guide the search toward the most informative regions of the scenario space. The article demonstrates the approach on several AV testing problems, showing substantial improvements in sample efficiency compared to uniform random sampling and standard importance sampling techniques. BAMS represents an important advance in making simulation-based ADS safety testing more tractable by focusing computational resources where they are most needed.

## Brake Onset Detection

**Authors:** J. Liu, K. D. Kusano, J. M. Scanlon, and T. Victor,

**Title:** "Automated brake onset detection from kinematics for naturalistic driving data," 

arXiv:2507.17943v2, 2025.

This article develops and validates automated algorithms for detecting the onset of braking from kinematic data recorded in naturalistic driving studies (NDS). Accurate identification of brake onset timing is critical for understanding driver behavior in pre-crash scenarios, estimating driver reaction times, and calibrating simulation models used in ADS safety evaluation. The authors compare multiple detection approaches—including threshold-based methods applied to longitudinal acceleration signals, change-point detection algorithms, and machine learning classifiers—against manually annotated ground truth from NDS video data. The article addresses the practical challenges of brake onset detection in real-world data, including sensor noise, road grade effects, and the gradual nature of many braking events that lack a clear, instantaneous onset. The resulting algorithms provide a scalable method for extracting brake onset timing from large NDS databases, enabling population-level analyses of driver braking behavior that would be infeasible through manual video annotation alone. This work supports the broader ADS safety evaluation ecosystem by providing more accurate human driver behavior models for use in counterfactual simulations that compare ADS performance to human performance.
